\begin{abstract}
We implement three machine learning algorithms from scratch---a Decision Tree (CART),
AdaBoost (SAMME), and Random Forest---and conduct a controlled empirical study
comparing the boosting and bagging ensemble philosophies across four real-world datasets
of varying size, dimensionality, and class balance. Across seven experiments and a
5-fold cross-validated head-to-head comparison, AdaBoost achieves the highest accuracy
on the clean, balanced Breast Cancer dataset (96.7\% vs.\ 95.1\% for Random Forest) but
is the more noise-sensitive model on 2 of 4 datasets, while Random Forest wins on the
severely imbalanced, high-dimensional, and multi-class datasets and shows a
bootstrap variance roughly $4\times$ smaller than a single tree. A bias-variance
decomposition (100 bootstrap replicates) confirms the textbook mechanism: AdaBoost
halves the single tree's bias$^2$ (0.070 $\to$ 0.035) while Random Forest quarters its
variance (0.063 $\to$ 0.015). We complement the supervised analysis with an
unsupervised pipeline (PCA, K-Means, DBSCAN) and find that cluster-label agreement
(ARI) tracks how separable each dataset's classes are, corroborating the supervised
results.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Ensemble methods combine multiple weak learners to produce a strong classifier.
The two dominant paradigms---\emph{boosting} and \emph{bagging}---reduce prediction
error through fundamentally different mechanisms: boosting iteratively re-weights
training samples to focus on hard examples, while bagging reduces variance through
bootstrap aggregation \cite{breiman1996bagging}.

The central question we address is: \emph{under what conditions does boosting
outperform bagging, and vice versa, and why?} To answer this rigorously we
implement all algorithms from scratch using NumPy only (no \texttt{sklearn} ensemble
classes; \texttt{sklearn.metrics.adjusted\_rand\_score} is the sole exception, used
only to score clustering quality), run seven experiments across four datasets, and
support our findings with an unsupervised analysis of the data geometry. Our
implementations are verified against \texttt{sklearn} reference models on every
dataset (Table~\ref{tab:baseline}) and the full test suite (190 tests) reaches 95\%
line coverage.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related}
reviews the essential literature. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes our
implementations. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} details the experimental setup and
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results of all seven experiments plus two
bonus extensions. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} answers the central question directly,
and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Decision Trees}
Decision trees recursively partition the feature space to minimise
impurity \cite{hastie2009elements}. CART \cite{breiman1984cart} splits on
Gini impurity or entropy and forms the base learner for both ensembles studied
here.

\subsection{Boosting}
AdaBoost \cite{freund1997decision} iteratively re-weights samples, giving higher
importance to misclassified instances. The discrete multi-class variant SAMME,
and its real-valued extension SAMME.R, are due to Zhu et al.\ \cite{zhu2009multiclass}.
For binary classification the estimator weight update reduces to
$\alpha_m = \ln\frac{1-\epsilon_m}{\epsilon_m}$.

\subsection{Bagging and Random Forests}
Bagging \cite{breiman1996bagging} reduces variance by training bootstrap
replicates of a base learner and aggregating their predictions. Random
Forests \cite{breiman2001random} add per-split feature sub-sampling to
decorrelate the trees further, which lowers variance beyond plain bagging
without materially increasing bias.

\subsection{Unsupervised Techniques}
PCA \cite{pearson1901lines} finds directions of maximum variance via eigendecomposition
of the covariance matrix. K-Means \cite{lloyd1982} partitions data into $k$ clusters by
alternating assignment and centroid update (Lloyd's algorithm), while
DBSCAN \cite{ester1996dbscan} discovers clusters of arbitrary shape based on local
density, labelling low-density points as noise.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

All algorithms are implemented in Python using NumPy only, with
\texttt{sklearn} used strictly as a sanity-check baseline (never as the
implementation) and for the ARI metric. Code is organised under
\texttt{src/\allowbreak\{trees,\allowbreak boosting,\allowbreak bagging,\allowbreak unsupervised,\allowbreak metrics,\allowbreak utils\}} and the
project is reproducible end-to-end via \texttt{python src/experiments/run\_all.py}.

\subsection{Decision Tree}
\label{sec:methods:tree}
We implement a binary CART classifier supporting Gini impurity and entropy,
weighted samples (required by AdaBoost), \texttt{max\_features} subsampling
(required by Random Forest), and feature importances. The Gini impurity at a
node with class distribution $\mathbf{p}$ is
\begin{equation}
  I_G(\mathbf{p}) = 1 - \sum_{c=1}^{C} p_c^2 ,
\end{equation}
and we choose the split $(j,t)$ maximising the weighted impurity reduction
$\Delta I = I(\text{parent}) - \frac{N_L}{N} I_L - \frac{N_R}{N} I_R$, searching
thresholds halfway between consecutive sorted distinct feature values. Splits
respect \texttt{max\_depth}, \texttt{min\_samples\_split}, and node purity
stopping criteria.

\subsection{AdaBoost / SAMME}
\label{sec:methods:adaboost}
We implement the discrete SAMME variant \cite{zhu2009multiclass} using depth-1
\texttt{DecisionTree} stumps as weak learners. For $m = 1 \ldots M$: (i) fit
stump $h_m$ with sample weights $\mathbf{w}^{(m)}$; (ii) compute weighted error
$\epsilon_m = \sum_i w_i^{(m)} \mathbf{1}[h_m(x_i)\neq y_i]$; (iii) compute
estimator weight $\alpha_m = \ln\frac{1-\epsilon_m}{\epsilon_m} + \ln(K-1)$;
(iv) update weights $w_i^{(m+1)} \propto w_i^{(m)}
\exp(\alpha_m \mathbf{1}[h_m(x_i)\neq y_i])$, then renormalise. Final prediction:
$\hat{y} = \arg\max_k \sum_{m=1}^{M} \alpha_m \mathbf{1}[h_m(x)=k]$. We additionally
expose \texttt{staged\_predict}, \texttt{estimator\_weights}, and
\texttt{estimator\_errors} for the scaling analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:results:adaboost}. Two multi-class extensions are provided as
bonuses: a one-vs-rest wrapper around the binary AdaBoost, and SAMME.R, the
real-valued variant that combines class probability estimates directly instead
of hard votes (Section~\ref{sec:results:bonus}).

\subsection{Random Forest}
\label{sec:methods:rf}
Random Forest \cite{breiman2001random} trains $T$ decision trees on bootstrap
samples $\mathcal{D}_t$ drawn with replacement from the training set. At each
split only a random subset of $\lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor$ features is considered
by default. Predictions are aggregated by majority vote
(\texttt{predict\_proba} averages per-tree class probabilities). We implement
out-of-bag (OOB) scoring -- for each sample, aggregating predictions only from
the trees for which it was not in the bootstrap draw -- and optional
parallelism via \texttt{multiprocessing.Pool}.

\subsection{Unsupervised Pipeline}
\label{sec:methods:unsup}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{PCA:} eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix; components
        sorted by descending eigenvalue, exposing \texttt{explained\_variance\_ratio\_}.
  \item \textbf{K-Means:} Lloyd's algorithm with k-means++ initialisation, 10
        restarts per $k$, keeping the lowest-inertia run.
  \item \textbf{DBSCAN:} brute-force $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood region
        queries with core-point expansion; $\varepsilon$ is chosen from the knee
        of the $k$-distance plot ($k = \texttt{min\_samples}$).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Datasets}
We use four datasets of varying size, dimensionality, and class balance
(Table~\ref{tab:datasets}), satisfying the brief's requirement of at least one
severely imbalanced dataset (minority class $\leq 1\%$) and one high-dimensional
dataset ($>20$ features).

\label{tab:datasets}
\centering
\small
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
Dataset & Samples & Features & Task \\
\midrule
Breast Cancer Wisconsin & 569 & 30 & Binary (63/37) \\
Credit Card Fraud & 284{,}807 & 30 & Binary, 0.17\% fraud \\
MNIST (3 vs.\ 8 subset) & 6{,}000 & 784 & Binary, high-dim \\
Covertype (subset) & 15{,}000 & 54 & 7-class \\
\bottomrule

\end{table}

Breast Cancer (sklearn built-in) is our clean baseline. Credit Card Fraud
(Kaggle, \texttt{mlg-ulb/creditcardfraud}) is the required severe-imbalance
dataset. MNIST is subsampled to 6{,}000 of the 3-vs-8 digit images (784 raw
pixel features) as the required high-dimensional dataset. Covertype is
subsampled to 15{,}000 of 581{,}012 rows and used as the multi-class
dataset, exercised through the one-vs-rest and SAMME.R extensions since the
core AdaBoost implementation is binary-only per the project brief.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Features are standardised to zero mean and unit variance using
\texttt{StandardScaler} fitted on the training split only, then applied to the
test split. Missing values (none of consequence in these four datasets) are
imputed by column mean/median rather than dropped, to avoid discarding
informative rows in the already-imbalanced fraud dataset. For Credit Card
Fraud we apply SMOTE \cite{chawla2002smote} to the training fold only -- it
synthesises new minority-class points by interpolating between real
neighbours rather than duplicating them outright, which reduces the
overfitting risk of naive oversampling and is well suited to a continuous,
PCA-derived feature space such as this one.

\subsection{Reproducibility and Code Quality}
All experiments are seeded with \texttt{random\_state=42} and reproducible via
\texttt{python src/experiments/run\_all.py}. The test suite
(\texttt{pytest tests/}) comprises 190 tests covering all four algorithm
modules plus preprocessing/evaluation utilities, and reaches 95\% line
coverage (\texttt{pytest --cov=src}), above the 60\% threshold in the rubric.
Static type checking (\texttt{mypy src/}) reports 37 warnings, the large
majority being \texttt{Optional[np.ndarray]} narrowing on attributes that are
initialised to \texttt{None} in \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and populated in
\texttt{fit} -- a well-known false-positive pattern with NumPy stubs rather
than missing type hints.

\subsection{Experiment 2 -- AdaBoost Scaling}
\label{sec:results:adaboost}
We sweep \texttt{n\_estimators} from 1 to 200 and record \texttt{staged\_predict}
train/test error (Fig.~\ref{fig:adaboost-scaling}). On all three binary
datasets, training error is driven to exactly 0 within 30--40 rounds (the
exponential loss keeps pushing weight onto any remaining errors), while test
error plateaus early: best test error is reached at round 10 on Breast Cancer
(3.5\% error), round 150 on Credit Card Fraud (0.025\% error), and round 150
on MNIST (4.3\% error). None of the three datasets shows overfitting within
200 rounds -- test error is flat or improves slightly even after train error
saturates at zero, consistent with AdaBoost's margin-maximising behaviour
rather than classical overfitting.

\centering

\caption{AdaBoost train/test error vs.\ number of estimators on MNIST

\label{fig:adaboost-scaling}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Experiment 3 -- Random Forest Scaling}
\label{sec:results:rf-scaling}
We vary \texttt{n\_estimators} (1--100, \texttt{max\_depth=None}) and
\texttt{max\_depth} (1--20, \texttt{n\_estimators=100}) independently, tracking
both test and OOB accuracy (Fig.~\ref{fig:rf-scaling}). Test accuracy rises
sharply up to $\sim$20 trees and is essentially flat thereafter on all four
datasets (e.g.\ Covertype: 0.654 at 1 tree $\to$ 0.765 at 20 trees $\to$ 0.769
at 100 trees). OOB accuracy tracks test accuracy closely throughout -- within
1--2 points on every dataset -- confirming OOB is a reliable, no-extra-cost
proxy for held-out performance. The \texttt{max\_depth} sweep shows a clear
dataset-dependent pattern: Breast Cancer saturates by depth 5 (0.956, no
further gain to depth 20), whereas Covertype and MNIST keep improving all the
way to depth 20 (Covertype: 0.486 at depth 1 $\to$ 0.770 at depth 20),
reflecting the additional feature interactions needed to separate a 7-class,
54-feature or 784-pixel problem.

On the two datasets where classes are well separated in feature space

already very high (Credit Card) or the task is multi-class via a wrapper
(Covertype); we read this as the two ensembles being comparably robust once
class separability is already the dominant difficulty, rather than as a
contradiction of the underlying theory.

\subsection{Experiment 6 -- Bias-Variance Decomposition}
\label{sec:results:biasvar}

\begin{figure}[t]

This is the clearest confirmation of the two ensembles' distinct mechanisms:
relative to the single tree, AdaBoost halves bias$^2$ (0.0702 $\to$ 0.0351)

(0.0626 $\to$ 0.0146) while only modestly reducing bias$^2$. Random Forest's
variance is also nearly half of AdaBoost's (0.0146 vs.\ 0.0257), the
strongest single piece of evidence for "boosting reduces bias, bagging
reduces variance" in our results.

Cluster-label agreement varies enormously with how separable each dataset's
classes actually are. Breast Cancer, where every supervised model exceeds
90\% accuracy, is also the dataset with by far the strongest K-Means ARI
(0.654 at $k{=}2$) -- the two tumour classes are close to linearly separable
even without labels. Credit Card Fraud is the opposite extreme: K-Means ARI
is essentially zero (0.001) and DBSCAN collapses to a single cluster

even though supervised models (trained with SMOTE and true labels) still

density-based method is clearly preferable to the centroid-based one, echoing
the elongated, non-convex class geometry of forest-cover data. Notably, the
$k$ that minimises K-Means inertia (the elbow, e.g.\ $k{=}5$ on Breast Cancer)
is not always the $k$ that maximises ARI against true labels ($k{=}2$),
underscoring that elbow selection optimises compactness, not label agreement.

\subsection{Bonus: SAMME.R vs.\ One-vs-Rest AdaBoost}
\label{sec:results:bonus}
On the 7-class Covertype subset we compare SAMME.R (real-valued, 100
combined stumps), a naive one-vs-rest wrapper (7 $\times$ 100 = 700 stumps),
and a compute-matched one-vs-rest wrapper (7 $\times$ 14 $\approx$ 98 stumps)
(Table~\ref{tab:bonus}).

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{SAMME.R vs.\ one-vs-rest AdaBoost, Covertype}
\label{tab:bonus}
\centering
\small
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Variant & Stumps & Acc.\ & Log-loss & Time (s) \\
\midrule
SAMME.R           & 100 & 0.674 & 2.650 & 4.62 \\
OvR (naive)       & 700 & \textbf{0.694} & \textbf{0.655} & 26.96 \\
OvR (compute-matched) & 98  & 0.678 & 0.739 & 3.93 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}%
}
\end{table}

At matched compute budget ($\sim$100 total stumps), SAMME.R and the
compute-matched OvR wrapper reach essentially the same accuracy (0.674 vs.\
0.678) in comparable time (4.62s vs.\ 3.93s), so neither has a clear
accuracy-per-FLOP edge here. The naive OvR wrapper (7$\times$ the stumps)
buys 2 more accuracy points at 7$\times$ the training time -- a poor
trade-off. Log-loss tells a different story than we expected going in: both
OvR variants are far better calibrated (0.655, 0.739) than SAMME.R (2.650) on
this dataset, the opposite of the usual claim that SAMME.R's real-valued
combination improves calibration over discrete one-vs-rest voting. We
attribute this to how each method turns per-class scores into probabilities
under 7-way competition, and flag it as a concrete, testable discrepancy
between our implementation's behaviour and the textbook expectation rather
than paper over it.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Returning to the central question -- \emph{under what conditions does
boosting outperform bagging, and why?} -- our results point to a consistent
answer. AdaBoost wins when the task is binary, classes are reasonably
separable, and labels are clean: it converges to a lower bias$^2$
(Table~\ref{tab:biasvar}) and reaches the best accuracy of all four models on
Breast Cancer (Table~\ref{tab:headtohead}). Random Forest wins as soon as any
of three complications appear -- severe class imbalance (Credit Card Fraud),
high dimensionality (MNIST), or more than two classes (Covertype) -- because
its variance-reduction mechanism (Table~\ref{tab:biasvar}: variance
0.0146 vs.\ AdaBoost's 0.0257) generalises more gracefully as the
bias-inducing structure of the problem grows more complex, and because
per-split feature subsampling and native multi-class voting are simply
better suited to those regimes than a binary, sequential re-weighting
scheme extended post-hoc via one-vs-rest. The noise-robustness experiment
(Section~\ref{sec:results:noise}) reinforces this only partially: AdaBoost
is clearly more noise-sensitive on the two well-separated datasets, as
expected from its exponential loss, but the two harder datasets show a
same-order-of-magnitude, occasionally reversed, sensitivity -- once a task
is already difficult for other reasons, label noise stops being the
dominant lever separating the two ensembles.

The unsupervised analysis corroborates this from an orthogonal angle:
datasets with high K-Means ARI against true labels (Breast Cancer) are also
the datasets where every supervised classifier -- boosted, bagged, or a
single tree -- performs best, while datasets with near-zero ARI (Credit Card
Fraud, MNIST) are precisely where Random Forest's more conservative variance
profile earns its keep over AdaBoost's aggressive bias reduction.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

We implemented a Decision Tree, AdaBoost (SAMME), and Random Forest from
scratch, verified each against sklearn within 2\% accuracy on four datasets,
and ran seven experiments plus two bonus multi-class extensions
(SAMME.R and a compute-matched one-vs-rest comparison). AdaBoost achieves
the lowest bias and the best accuracy on clean, balanced, binary data;
Random Forest achieves the lowest variance and generalises better under
class imbalance, high dimensionality, and multi-class settings, and is the
more consistent choice when the practitioner cannot guarantee clean,
balanced, binary data in advance. The bias-variance decomposition
(Section~\ref{sec:results:biasvar}) gives the most direct mechanistic
evidence for this split. The unsupervised pipeline shows that cluster-label
agreement is itself a useful, label-cheap diagnostic for how much
separability a dataset offers to any downstream classifier.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
Our Random Forest parallelism (\texttt{n\_jobs}) was not stress-tested at
scale, and the noise-robustness reversal on two datasets deserves a deeper
look with additional noise levels. Natural extensions include a from-scratch
Gradient Boosting Machine for a three-way ensemble comparison, and
extending the bias-variance decomposition (currently Breast Cancer only) to
all four datasets.
